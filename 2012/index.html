Dear Delegates and Faculty Advisors,

3 hectic days. 27 hours of conferencing. Many sleepless nights. One great success.
Our primary objective was to provide the delegates with the best possible environment to reach consensus on some of the most relevant and heated topics today. The response was far beyond what we had ever imagined.
Here is a short sum-up of the committee sessions:
Debating it out diplomatically, the Disarmament and International Security Committee was able to successfully reach a consensus on two crises; one regarding the NATO attack on Afghanistan and the other regarding the North Korean dispatch of nuclear missiles to South Korea. The delegates quickly lobbied and merged and eventually a resolution was passed with Afghanistan, India, North Korea and Uganda as the main submitters.
In the Human Rights Commission an agreement was reached by member states on the issue of rehabilitation of child soldiers and censorship and freedom of expression in the light of the new media. The crisis the Commission encountered was the assassination of the President of the Sri Lankan civil society. The Commission efficiently worked together to draw up a successful resolution.


The Economic and Social Council, on the very first day, was faced with the challenge of coming up with a resolution that would address India’s unhealthy air pollution levels. Not only was a remarkable resolution presented, but the Council voted with overwhelming majority for the resolution and it was successfully adopted.
Not to be outdone, the members of the Security Council debated passionately to reach a consensus on the issues at hand. I must say, there was never a dull moment. With the USA and China both determined not to back down, we observers were apprehensive that a resolution would ever be passed. However, the Council found an effective solution to the set agenda of ‘Right to Intervene’. The Security Council was also challenged with another crisis on the second day. The crisis was NATO airstrikes on Palestine and Syria. After heated debates and recurrent flirtations with Veto, a comprehensive resolution was unanimously agreed upon. This resolution effectively calls for the implementation of the two-state-solution in the Middle East and the creation of an internationally recognised State of Palestine.


The topics set and the crises that were introduced really tested the ability of the delegates to think on their feet. I must mention that there were a few delegates who really stepped up to the challenge and although under tremendous pressure, were able to keep calm and reach an agreeable solution.
The conference would not have been made possible if it weren’t for our dedicated team of volunteers. From the Chief Guests, Principal and Vice-Principals for their constant support, Faculty Advisors, Teachers, Director General, Support Staff, Operations Team, Training and Research team, Media Team, Committee Heads and Co-Heads, Special Committee Officials, Pages, to the rest of the CAMUN Officials who worked tirelessly to make sure the session ran smoothly. Last but not the least, all the delegates, from Al Ain Junior British School, Modern High School, Our Own English High School and the Cambridge High School who represented their countries with such fervour and passion that they literally brought the House down.


To everyone who attended, thank you for the wonderful memories and contributing to the "best-ever" CAMUN conference so far. 

All in all, it was truly an event to remember. 
On a final note, I strongly believe the conference also taught us that it is possible for countries to work together and find solutions that are in the interests of all nations. I believe it has also taught us that if we are determined never to give up, we can constantly challenge ourselves to find opportunities and solutions that help make our world a better place. Our voice matters. Let us not get complacent and settle for anything less but relentlessly strive to make each new day a better one for the next generation. As tomorrow’s leaders, we have within us the ability to create the future.
Circumstances should never define us; rather how we respond to them should.
Hope to see you all next year,



Priya Anne George
Secretary-General
Cambridge Model United Nations 2012
